INSTRUMENTS

The instruments at Jaipur's Jantar Mantar function through geometric precision to observe celestial bodies and measure time. Most instruments use shadows cast by the sun or alignments of celestial objects to calculate positions and movements.

The astronomical observatory featuring 19 large instruments.

Notable instruments include:

  1. 1. Samrat Yantra – The Giant Sundial
  2. 2. Jai Prakash Yantra – Hemispherical Altitude Instrument
  3. 3. Rashivalaya – Zodiac Instrument
  4. 4. Rama Yantra – Azimuth and Altitude Measurement
  5. 5. Dakshin Bhitti Yantra – Meridian Instrument
  6. 6. Digamsa Yantra – Azimuth and Weather Instrument

Samrat Yantra – The Giant Sundial

Measures Time

The Samrat Yantra, meaning "Supreme Instrument," is the largest sundial in the world, standing at an impressive 27 meters high. It measures time with unparalleled accuracy—down to two seconds—and helps determine local solar time, or the time when the sun reaches its zenith.

The steep angle and massive size allow for precise readings of Jaipur’s time, making it one of the most significant instruments at Jantar Mantar.

The shadow cast by the gnomon on the sundial’s quadrants moves every minute, making this instrument a marvel in timekeeping.

Jai Prakash Yantra – Hemispherical Altitude Instrument

Measures Altitude

The Jai Prakash Yantra consists of two hemispherical structures with markings that map the sky. Using this instrument, astronomers could determine the altitude of celestial bodies like stars, planets, and the sun.

It features cross-wires stretched over the hemispherical structure, which help pinpoint the exact position of objects in the sky.

By projecting the celestial sphere onto the interior surface of the bowl, this instrument provides precise measurements and offers a unique way to study the night sky.

Rashivalaya – Zodiac Instrument

Measures Ecliptic Coordinates of Constellations

The Rashivalaya is a specialised instrument designed to observe the ecliptic coordinates of the 12 zodiac constellations. It consists of 12 different devices, one for each sign of the zodiac.
The Rashivalaya helps track the movement of these constellations in relation to the Earth’s orbit and is used to calculate the celestial coordinates for each zodiac sign.

This instrument highlights the deep connection between astronomy and astrology in ancient India, where the study of stars and planets was used to predict celestial events and understand cosmic patterns.

Rama Yantra – Azimuth and Altitude Measurement

Measures Azimuth and Altitude

The Rama Yantra is a cylindrical instrument used to measure the altitude and azimuth (the horizontal angle) of celestial objects. It features two large circular walls with open spaces, allowing for precise alignment with stars and planets.

By using the vertical and horizontal lines marked on the instrument, astronomers could accurately determine the position of celestial bodies in the sky.

This tool, with its impressive design, reflects the scientific innovation of the time, providing detailed observations of the heavens.

Dakshin Bhitti Yantra – Meridian Instrument

Measures Meridian, Altitude, and Zenith Distances

The Dakshin Bhitti Yantra is a wall-based instrument used to observe the celestial meridian and measure the altitude and zenith distances of celestial objects. Positioned along the meridian line, this instrument helps track the movement of the sun and other objects as they cross the celestial equator.

By measuring the zenith distances (the angle between an object and the zenith, or directly overhead), it aids in calculating the latitude and time of observations with great precision.

Digamsa Yantra – Azimuth and Weather Instrument

Measures Azimuth, Sunrise/Sunset Time, and Weather Forecasting

The Digamsa Yantra is a circular instrument used to determine the azimuth (the direction of the sun) as well as the exact times of sunrise and sunset. This instrument is also valuable for weather forecasting, as it helps predict seasonal changes based on the sun’s movements.

The Digamsa Yantra’s simple yet effective design makes it an important tool for understanding daily solar cycles and changes in weather patterns, further emphasising the integration of astronomy and everyday life in ancient India.
