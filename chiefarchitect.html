CHIEF ARCHITECT OF THE JANTAR MANTAR

Imagine a young scholar in the early 1700s in India who could read and write in Sanskrit, Arabic and Persian. Then imagine a mathematician and architect. Pandit Jagannath Samrat, chief advisor to Sawai Jai Singh II was all this and more – he was also as astronomer.

“Sawai Jai Singh, shortly after his ascension was summoned to the imperial Mughal camp in the Deccan….While in the Deccan, one day, after one such campaign, Jai Singh was riding back to camp with his men. On the way, in one of the villages that he passed through, Jai Singh met a young Brahmin scholar, Jagannath, the son of Ganesa. Pandit Jagannath came from a traditional family of astronomers. So impressed was Sawai Jai Sing with the young Pandit that he not only requested him to accompany him to his camp, but also persuaded him to come north with him to Amber, even bestowing the title of ‘Samrat’ on him in deference to his supreme learning.”

Mukherji, A.S., 2010. Jantar Mantar, Maharaja Sawai Jai Singh’s Observatory in Delhi. Ambi Knowledge Resource. P13

Jagannath Samrat was instrumental in the construction of the Jantar Mantar. He chose the site in Jaipur, had the ground levelled, identified the directions and set about designing it, along with his equally erudite ruler, Jai Singh.

Jagannath was fascinated by Euclid, the Greek mathematician (from circa 300 BC) and the Father of Geometry. He translated Euclid's Elements into Sanskrit in 1719 (from the Arabic translation of Elements by Nasir al-Din al-Tusi) in a work titled Rekhāgaṇita.

Jai Singh had a collection of astrolabes, each exquisitely crafted. He understood their value in the study of astronomy and commissioned four manuals on astrolabes and other instruments. Jagannath Samrat compiled the first of these manuals, Yantraprakāra, on various astronomical instruments, measurements, computations and observations. While the Arabic and Persian works were in verse form, Jai Singh preferred prose for the translated works.

Vidya Sagar Bhattacharya was the chief architect of Jaipur. He was well-versed in urban planning and architecture and designed the city to be symmetrical with wide arterial roads with intersecting streets and lanes at right angles. The city’s layout was designed according to Vastu Shastra, meaning the science of architecture¸ a Hindu system of design that balances the five elements of nature and elements from astronomy and astrology. It emphasizes harmony and prosperity. Jaipur had temples, gardens, havelis and civic buildings devoted to specific activities. They were also forts and resting places for travellers.
