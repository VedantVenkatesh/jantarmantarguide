THE VISIONARY

By the time Jai Singh commissioned the construction of the Jantar Mantar in Jaipur between 1727 and 1734, the Mughal empire was witnessing a period of transition. Aurangzeb had passed away in 1707, and the Mughal empire was beginning to fragment under the weight of regional powers. Jai Singh strategically maintained ties with the Mughal court. He was appointed as a governor of key regions, including Malwa and Agra.

Jai Singh combined Rajput architectural styles with cutting-edge scientific knowledge from around the world, making his observatories an advanced astronomical centre of its time, unique and without parallel. They allowed people to observe celestial bodies, calculate time, and predict eclipses with unprecedented precision, which were used to determine the onset of the monsoons (important in an agrarian economy), dates of eclipses, auspicious dates for travel and ceremonies, and other dates that were important in the administration of the princely state.

There are several reasons Jai Singh used large instruments. He and his astronomers found that their instruments were able to calculate positions of moons and celestial bodies with far greater accuracy than the astrolabes and other instruments. In 1783, Colonel T.D. Pearse found in India a copy of a Persian book from the 5th century titled, “The Wonders of Creation.” The book contained records of the 7the satellite of Saturn. Until then, the western world was aware of only 5 satellites of Saturn. The existence of the 7 satellites of Saturn were confirmed in 1789 by Friedrich Wilhelm Herschel using a telescope of 40 feet focal length.

Jai Singh explains his approach in the Zij-i-Muhammad Shahi,

“Since the telescope is not readily available to an average man, we are going to base our rules of computation for naked eye observations only, which in turn, are based on earlier texts.”

Dr. V.N. Sharma, a scholar who has extensively studied the Jantar Mantar opines that several of the instruments were designed by Jai Singh and are original, without parallel or precedent.

Accredited Architects and Contributors

There were various influences on Jai Singh that eventually led to the making of the Jantar Mantar. He reached out to mathematicians, astronomers, historians and geometricians from all over the world.

Jagannath Samrat

Chief Architect, Samrat advised on astronomy and design.

Vidyadhar Bhattacharya

Chief architect and planner of the city of Jaipur.

Mohammed Sharif and Mohammed Mahdi

They were deputed by Jai Singh II to acquire manuscripts of great medieval Muslim astronomers. They also collected astrolabes and other instruments used in the study of astronomy.

Pere Manoel de Figueiredo

King Emmanuel of Portugal sent European scientist, Pere Manoel de Figueiredo to Jaipur in ~1730, equipped with several books and instruments. Jai Singh had reached out to the Viceroy of Goa and sought his assistance in approaching King Emmanuel.

Pedro Da Sulva

A physician deputed to Jaipur by King Emmanuel of Portugal, along with Pere Manoel de Figueiredo.

Antonne Gabelsperger

A Bavarian Jesuit joined Andre Strobl.

Father Andre Strobl and Claude Boudier

Two French Jesuit scholars were invited to Jaipur to advise on astronomy and scientific developments in Europe. Andre Stroble reached Goa in 1740.
