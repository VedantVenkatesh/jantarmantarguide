STORY OF THE JANTAR MANTAR

The Jantar Mantar has a compelling presence in Jaipur. Its history feels palpable to those who explore its well-organized instruments. Each yantra or instrument has a story to tell and at every turn, there is something to transport you back in time.

The genesis of the Jantar Mantar can be found in the Zij-i-Muhammad Shahi, Jai Singh’s astronomical tables published in 1733. Jai Singh, writing in the third person about himself, states:

“The calculation of the places of the stars, as obtained from the tables and common news in many cases, give them widely different positions by those determined by observation, especially in the appearance of the new moons. Seeing that very important affairs, both regarding religion and the administration of the empire, depend upon these…he represented the matter to the Emperor Muhammad Shah.”

Muhammad Shah was appointed as Emperor in 1719 in Delhi with a formal coronation in 1720. Jai Singh met him and discussed, among other issues, the concept of an observatory to precisely calculate position of stars, timing of eclipses, etc

The Zij-i-Muhammad Shahi contains Muhammad Shah’s response to Jai Singh:

“Since you, who are learned in the mysteries of science, have a perfect knowledge of this matter, having assembled the astronomers and geometrician of the faith of Islam and the Brahmans and Pandits and the astronomers of Europe, and having prepared all the apparatus of an observatory – do you so labor for the ascertaining of the point in question, that the disagreement between the calculated times of those phenomena and the times they are observed to happen, may be rectified.”

The Samrāṭ Siddhānta also mentions how Jai Singh, who earlier used astrolabes made of metal increasingly realized their limitations, thereby setting the foundation for a large outdoor observatory that became the Jantar Mantar.

Materials used to build the Jantar Mantar

The primary material used to make the Jantar Mantar is red sandstone. Marble, bronze, stone and bricks are also used. The Persian -Arabic school of astronomy used brass instruments to track time, altitude and position of celestial bodies. Astrolabes used by Mughal rulers (some of which may be viewed at the Humayun Tomb Museum and the National Museum in Delhi), were made of brass. Jai Singh II realized that brass instruments were subject to wear and tear, as a result, displacing the centre of these instruments, in turn shifting the plan of reference. They were no longer precision instruments, something that Jai Singh valued. This prompted him to explore other materials such as red sandstone, which was easily available in Rajasthan and would stand the test of time.

Jai Singh writes in the Zij-i-Muhammad Shahi:

“But finding that brass instruments did not come up to ideas which he had formed of accuracy, because of the smallness of the size, the want of division into minutes, the shaking and wearing of their axes, the shifting of the planes of the instruments, etc…. he constructed in Delhi instruments of his own invention, of storm and line of perfect stability.”
