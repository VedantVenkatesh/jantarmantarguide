Maharaja
Sawai Jai Singh

Sawai Jai Singh II, or Jai Singh as he is commonly known today, is one of Rajasthan’s most celebrated rulers. The name “Jai”means victory, while “Singh”, which translates to lion, symbolises bravery—a fitting title for a ruler from the valiant Rajput warrior clan. The honorific “Sawai,” meaning "one and a quarter" in Sanskrit, was officially bestowed upon him in 1712 to signify that he was one and a quarter times superior to his peers. To commemorate this, the rulers of Jaipur began the tradition of flying two flags, one full-sized and the other a quarter-sized.

Born on 3 November 1688, to Maharaja Bishan Singh of Amer and his Rathor queen, Indra Kumari, Jai Singh ascended the throne at the age of 12 following his father's death. With the support of experienced advisors, he led Rajasthan from his new capital in Jaipur until his death on 21 September 1743, at the age of 55. During his reign, he founded the city of Jaipur and built five astronomical observatories, the Jantar Mantar in Jaipur being the grandest.

Jai Singh the Scholar

Jai Singh II was not only a ruler but also an avid scholar with a deep love for knowledge. He was well-versed in Sanskrit, science, astronomy, architecture, literature, mathematics, art, and economics. His court was a vibrant hub of intellectuals and scholars proficient in the Dharma Shastras (ancient Indian jurisprudence), grammar, rituals, and astronomy. Jai Singh had several works translated from Arabic into Sanskrit and his extensive library contained works by renowned scholars such as Aryabhatt, Brahmagupta, Bhaskaracharya, Euclid, Ptolemy, and Nasir al-Din al-Tusi.

Jai Singh the Astronomer

Jai Singh's passion for astronomy shaped his legacy. Deeply invested in the latest advancements in the field, he commissioned the construction of five observatories, including the Jantar Mantar in Jaipur, all featuring original and highly accurate astronomical instruments. His personal library included influential works like John Flamsteed's Historia Coelestis Britannica, the Portuguese astronomer, Pere de la Hire’s Tabulae Astronomicae, Turkish astronomer, Ulugh Beg's Zij Ulugh Begi, and the ancient Greek scholar Ptolemy's Almagest. Jai Singh also composed his own astronomical treatise, Brahma Bodhini, demonstrating his scholarly contributions to the field.

Jai Singh the Statesman

Politically astute and diplomatic, Jai Singh II maintained a close relationship with Emperor Aurangzeb until the latter's death in 1707. After a period of political unrest, Jai Singh emerged as a key figure in the Mughal court, establishing strong ties with Emperor Muhammad Shah, who ruled from 1719 to 1748. Under Muhammad Shah’s patronage, Jai Singh secured permission to build the five Jantar Mantar observatories across northern India. In honour of the emperor, Jai Singh titled his collection of astronomical tables Zij-e-Muhammad-Shahi.
