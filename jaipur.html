Jaipur, the capital city of Rajasthan is a vibrant metropolis known as the Pink City, due to its distinctively coloured buildings. Founded in 1727, it is named Jaipur after its founder-ruler, Sawai Jai Singh II.

Jaipur was meticulously planned, blending Rajputana traditions with scientific principles.

Vidya Sagar Bhattacharya was the chief architect of Jaipur. He was well-versed in urban planning and architecture and designed the city to be symmetrical with wide arterial roads with intersecting streets and lanes at right angles. The city’s layout was designed according to Vastu Shastra, meaning the science of architecture¸ a Hindu system of design that balances the five elements of nature and elements from astronomy and astrology. It emphasizes harmony and prosperity. Jaipur had temples, gardens, havelis and civic buildings devoted to specific activities. They were also forts and resting places for travellers.

There are numerous reports of Jaipur and its architectural beauty. An 1832 account of Jaipur and Amber by a French scholar reads,

“The main streets are the principle bazaars; on each side, under the arcades of the palaces, temples, and houses, are the shops of the artisans, who are seen working almost in the open air at the trades, the Tailors, Shoemakers, Goldsmiths, armourers, pastry-cooks, confectioners, coppersmiths, etc. The merchants occupy very spacious, hot hatch, constructed a sort of course let work, in the midst of the main streets.”

Jaipur was designed with 8 gates – named after the Sun, Moon, North Star, two Hindu deities, Ram and Shiv, the Ajmeri Gate (that leads to Ajmer that houses a sacred shrine revered in Islam) and Ganga and Naya.

The City Palace, Hawa Mahal, and Amber Fort stand as magnificent reminders of the architectural prowess of the Kachwaha dynasty. These monuments are more than just buildings – they are symbols of a dynasty that nurtured art, culture, and scholarship

Jaipur’s cultural influence flourished under the patronage of its rulers, with grand festivals, intricate jewellery, and elaborate textile craftsmanship reflecting the city’s royal roots. The Kachwaha kings, known for their architectural ingenuity, developed Jaipur into a city of elegance and grandeur, which continues to attract visitors from around the world.

Today, Jaipur’s royal heritage is not just preserved in its palaces and forts, but also in the traditions, music, and art that continue to thrive in the modern city. Every corner of Jaipur tells a story – of its kings, their ambitions, and their enduring impact on Indian culture.

Jaipur’s Strategic Importance to the Mughals

The route from Delhi to Ajmer was through Jaipur and access to Ajmer was important for the Mughals. Ajmer is home to shrine or dargah of one of Islam’s most revered saint and scholar, Mu'in al-Din Hasan Chishti Sijzi. He was a Sufi preacher in the early 13th century and founder of the Chishtiyya mysticism of the Sunni order. It is said that Muhammad bin Tughluq (reigned from 1324 to 1351) visited the tomb in 1332 and Mughal Emperor Akbar visited the shrine 14 times during his reign. When Chishti died, his tomb in Ajmer became a venerated site, accessible only through Jaipur from Delhi (or Shahjahanabad).
